Couples Repair Toolkit

Every couple has ruptures. What separates thriving couples from struggling ones isn't whether they fight — it's how well they repair afterward. This toolkit walks you through the four steps.

Start here: Both partners need to be below a heart rate of 100 bpm before any meaningful repair is possible. If either of you is still flooded, skip to Step 1 first. The words below won't land until your nervous systems are regulated.
1
Cool Down
Take a real break — at least 20 minutes

When a fight escalates, your heart rate climbs above 100 bpm and your prefrontal cortex — the part that reasons, empathizes, and problem-solves — effectively goes offline. Anything either of you says or hears at that point will be distorted by the threat response. You can't repair what you can't think clearly about.

The break needs to be at least 20 minutes — that's how long it takes for cortisol levels to begin returning to baseline. Both partners must agree to pause and commit to a specific time to return.

John Gottman's research shows that physiological flooding is one of the strongest predictors of relationship decline. Couples who learn to take breaks and return to the conversation have dramatically better outcomes than those who push through when flooded.
Scripts to call a break
"I'm getting flooded. I need 30 minutes to calm down so I can actually hear you. Can we come back to this at [specific time]?"
"I want to work through this with you, but I'm not in a good place to talk right now. I'm going to [take a walk / be alone for a bit]. Let's talk at [time]."
"I'm not abandoning this conversation — I just need a pause. This matters to me and I want us to do it right."
2
Acknowledge Each Other
Validate before you explain

Before either of you explains your side, both of you need to feel heard. Validation doesn't mean agreeing — it means saying "I can see why you felt that way" even if you see it differently. Most conversations collapse here because both partners are trying to be understood before they've tried to understand.

This step is not about who was right. It's about both of you saying: your feelings made sense given what you experienced.

Sue Johnson's Emotionally Focused Therapy research shows that the primary drive in conflict is almost always an attachment signal — one partner feeling unseen, unsafe, or alone. When that signal gets acknowledged, the nervous system relaxes enough to actually connect.
Scripts to open acknowledgment
"I want to understand your side before I share mine. What was going on for you when this started?"
"What I heard you saying was [reflect back]. Is that right? I want to make sure I'm getting it."
"I can see why that felt [hurtful / confusing / unfair] to you. I didn't intend it that way, and I'm sorry it landed like that."
"I got reactive and it wasn't fair to you. Here's what was going on for me — not as an excuse, just so you understand."
3
Make a Repair Attempt
Reach toward each other — imperfectly is fine

A repair attempt is any gesture — verbal or physical — that says: "I want to stay connected with you through this." It doesn't have to be eloquent. It can be clumsy. What matters is the intent: I'm choosing us over being right.

The goal isn't to resolve every grievance right now. It's to say: I see you, I'm still here, and I'd rather repair than stay stuck.

Gottman found that the success of a repair attempt has almost nothing to do with how skillful the attempt is — it depends on the emotional climate of the relationship. Couples who consistently show appreciation and affection are far more likely to accept a clumsy repair attempt than couples with a chronic negativity deficit.
Repair attempt scripts
"I don't want to fight with you. I love you and I want us to figure this out together."
"I'm sorry. Not the kind of sorry that ends the conversation — the kind that means I actually get what happened."
"Can I try again? I don't think I said what I actually meant, and I want to."
"I know we're not fully resolved. I still want you to know I'm on your side, even when we disagree."
"What do you need from me right now to feel okay between us?"
"I hear you. Even if we see it differently, I understand why it hurt. That matters."
4
Reconnect
Close the loop — restore safety and warmth

Repair isn't complete until you've re-established connection. That can be physical (a hug, a hand on the arm, sitting close) or verbal — a moment that signals to both of your nervous systems: we're okay. We're still us.

This step also includes acknowledging what the fight was actually about — not the surface argument, but the underlying need or fear. When you can name the deeper thing, you prevent the same fight from cycling back in a week.

Joree Rose's mindfulness-based approach emphasizes that reconnection is a somatic process as much as a verbal one. Physical touch releases oxytocin, which directly counteracts cortisol and signals safety to the nervous system. Even a brief moment of physical closeness can shift the emotional climate faster than words.
Scripts to close the loop
"I'm glad we talked. I love you. [Hug / hand / touch]."
"I think what this was really about, for me, was [deeper need/fear]. Is that true for you too, or something different?"
"I don't want to leave this with any residue between us. Are we okay?"
"We don't have to solve everything today. What I want you to know is: I choose you, even in the hard parts."
"Something I appreciate about how you handled this today: [specific thing]. I wanted you to know that."
